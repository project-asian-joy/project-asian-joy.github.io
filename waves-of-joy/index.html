About the Fellowship

The Waves of Joy Public Writing Fellowship is designed to nurture the voices of Asian American womxn writers working in short nonfiction, empowering them to share personal narratives, social commentary, and reflections in concise, accessible, and impactful ways. Whether through blogs, personal essays, press releases, letters to the editor, op-eds, interviews, magazine features, newspaper articles, or newsletters, this fellowship nurtures the storytellers who are changing the stories we tell ourselves and the world.

Eligibility

  • Identify as an Asian or Asian American womxn
  • Committed and passionate about writing
  • Want to grow and expand as a writer
  • Reside in Massachusetts
  • Able to meet in the Boston area on specified dates

Writers of all levels are welcome to apply. Prior publications are not required, applicants should demonstrate a genuine desire to grow and evolve as a writer within the public realm. While public writing can take many forms, the fellowship is focused on making writing accessible for readers with busy lives. So, it’s not intended for those aiming to write short nonfiction books.

How will Fellows be selected?

The eligibility criteria are just the starting point. What really matters to us when reviewing your application is getting a sense of who you are through your writing and understanding what drives you. What are you passionate about? And why is this fellowship important to you?

Who you are matters more than how well you write.

As you choose your writing sample, keep in mind that it doesn’t have to be published. Select or write a sample piece that showcases your voice and ability to engage with an audience. Help us see you as a public writer and understand the story you’re eager to tell.

Additionally, when selecting the cohort, we’re thinking about how well fellows’ personalities, interests, and identities complement one another. It’s not just about individual talent—it’s about creating a group who can come together and support each other’s growth.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is public writing?

Public writing is any writing meant for a wide audience. Unlike academic or personal writing, it’s made to be accessible and engaging for all kinds of readers.

Public writing is a powerful tool that shapes how we see the world, challenging dominant narratives and driving social change.

What are some key characteristics of public writing?

  • Broad Audience: Public writing is for everyone, so it needs to be easy to understand for people with all kinds of backgrounds and knowledge.
  • Clear and Concise: For writing to be easy to understand, it should be simple and to the point, avoiding jargon or complicated language.
  • Purposeful: It usually has a clear goal—whether that's to inform, persuade, entertain, or spark thought.
  • Engaging: Good public writing grabs attention and keeps readers interested, often using storytelling or other techniques to make it more compelling.
What are examples of short-form public writing?

Public writing can take many forms, including but not limited to:

  • Newspaper articles
  • Magazine articles
  • Blog posts
  • Social media posts
  • Newsletters
  • Public speeches
  • Letters to the editor
  • Op-eds
  • Press releases
  • Micro-memoirs
What’s the difference between personal and public writing?

This blog post says it best:

  • Personal writing is for the person doing the writing.
  • Personal writing is too specific to one situation to translate well to anyone else’s situation.
  • Personal writing helps those close to the event make some sense of it.
  • Personal writing has a specific purpose and needs no other justification.
  • Personal writing is usually emotional, passionate, and tunnel-visioned.
  • Public writing requires time to gestate and develop from the initial writing that generated it.
  • Public writing is shaped for the readers, not for the person writing it.
  • Public writing takes the concrete details of a single, personal experience to generate a discussion of the more universal experience readers will relate to.
Where will the fellowship meet?

We’re still finalizing the details, as finding free community spaces can be challenging. The goal is to avoid spending money on venues so that we pay fellows more. For this inaugural year, meetings will be held at locations that are easily accessible by public transportation.

Parking may be limited at some meeting spots, so we strongly encourage fellows to use public transit whenever possible.

Will food be provided?

The plan is to offer snacks in the form of baos or other at each meeting, as 2.5 hours can be a long time to go without food.

For the wrap up, dinner will be provided.

What’s the writing deliverable for an organization or cause required for Fellows?

Fellows will apply what they’ve learned throughout the program by creating a deliverable that suits the unique needs of an organization or cause. This could include a press release, legislation brief, educational one-pager, or public service announcement (PSA). By tailoring their writing to fit the needs of the organization or cause, fellows gain hands-on experience in adapting their skills for real-world impact.

Fellows have the flexibility to choose the organization or cause they wish to support, as long as the topic or outcome is relevant to the Asian American community.
