The Thousand Bloom

The Thousand Bloom derives its name from a technique that cultivates a single chrysanthemum plant to produce an abundance of flowers on one stem. Its symbolism of unity and collective strength is both beautiful and powerful, reflecting a community's long history of solidarity and resilience while celebrating the interconnectedness and enduring spirit that define Chinatown.

Significance of Chrysanthemums

The chrysanthemum blooms vibrantly in the fall, thriving when most flowers wither, symbolizing resilience and vitality in the face of adversity. In China, the chrysanthemum is celebrated during the Double Ninth Festival (Chongyang Festival), a time associated with honoring the elderly and paying tribute to the deceased. The festival, whose name sounds like "longevity" in Chinese, typically involves activities such as climbing, enjoying Chongyang cakes, admiring chrysanthemums, and caring for the elderly.

The Process

All 1,000 silk flowers are dyed with weld flower, assembled into bouquets of nine, and arranged along the fence at Reggie Wong Park over nine days to create a larger chrysanthemum silhouette. As the shape slowly takes form, the display vividly illustrates how beauty—much like Chinatown’s evolving narrative—unfolds over time, and how resilience is cultivated gradually, rather than achieved in a single day.

Key Concepts

Silk

Originating from China, silk is deeply rooted in tradition and instills a sense of cultural pride. It has come to symbolize prosperity and power.

Natural Dye

Capturing nature’s essence, these naturally dyed silk flowers bring the outdoors inside, reflecting the festival’s reverence for natural beauty and cultural traditions.

Chrysanthemum Tea

Served in Chinese restaurants, chrysanthemum tea invites family and friends to unwind, share stories, and celebrate traditions together. This simple ritual deepens connections and honors cherished customs.

Public Square Dancing (China)

Public square dancing fosters community spirit and celebrates vitality, embracing the festival’s themes of longevity and joy. It combines tradition with lively, inclusive social interaction.

Third Space

The festival offers a communal space where people gather beyond their routines, fostering social interaction, celebrating traditions, and strengthening community bonds through shared experiences.

Time Capsule

To preserve memories of this one-time event, a puzzle capturing the community's hopes and vision for Chinatown 99 years from now ensures that future generations remember this celebration.

the artist

Anita Yip

Anita Yip is a multidisciplinary artist whose works honor and uplift underheard voices and cultures through documentary photography, journalism, curated experiences, and new media. Exploring the intricate relationships between art, archives, and identity within the interplay of cultural truths and fictions, she poses the question: "What is remembered, and what is lost?" Her art confronts and mediates painful experiences, lack of representation, and scarcity or even the absence of resources.

Embracing the symbolism of the thousand bloom—an emblem of unity and collective strength that reflects Boston Chinatown’s long history of solidarity and resilience—Anita is making her public art debut with a project that celebrates both joy and perseverance.

The Thousand Bloom

The Thousand Bloom is a public art project featuring 1,000 silk chrysanthemums to symbolize Boston Chinatown’s resilience, incorporating a grand installation, business support initiatives, and Chinatown’s first ever Double Ninth Festival.
